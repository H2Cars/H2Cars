Infrastructure

As seen in the economics of hydrogen and hydrogen cars, these cars must be comparable in price to conventional cars in order to be a viable alternative. However, infrastructure allowing refueling and transportation of H2 is also beneficial to the success of hydrogen cars because of unlimited aspect it would give to hydrogen cars.

Learn more below 

Refueling stations

Having a similar range to conventional cars, hydrogen cars require similar infrastructure as observed by traditional diesel/gasoline vehicles. According to a 2017 survey by HyARC , there were 280 hydrogen refueling stations around the world -- mainly in California, Japan and Germany. Indeed, this is a small amount of stations, but it corresponds to the amount of fuel cell cars on the road today (about 10,000). There are reasons for hope though; as of the beginning of 2017, 140 new stations were being planned or under construction. For these stations to succeed, it is also important to consider how they will be built. Will they be built on existing refueling stations (provided that there is space for the storage facilities and delivery of hydrogen) or will they be built entirely from scratch?
The stations must also include other components that are crucial to dispensing H2 into cars. These include low-pressure storages, compressors, pre-cooling systems (used so that hydrogen does not heat up too much during the refuleling process) and the dispensers. The amount of these components needs to be carefully taken into account in order to properly meet demand.
Heavy investing will be required in the earlier stages as the number of refueling stations will have to keep pace with the increase in the amount of FCVs being purchased by consumers.

Transportation of H2

H2 transportation is of key importance to the viability of H2 vehicles, as H2 must be available at the refueling station. Indeed, H2 can be produced on site, but in the majority of cases, H2 is transported to a refueling station. In order to transport it, H2 must be compressed and stored in primarily one of two different ways: compressed gaseous hydrogen and liquefied hydrogen. Liquefied hydrogen is typically transferred by semi-truck, which is more cost-effective for long distances; whereas compressed gaseous hydrogen is transferred by pipeline. The use of a pipeline for transporting hydrogen would make sense if hydrogen were used as one of our primary energy sources; however, at the moment, this would not make sense since the investment required to make such pipelines would represent an enormous cost that is unjustified by existing demand. Hydrogen pipelines do exist, but they are far from ubiquitous. For example, most of these pipelines are located in either South-Eastern Texas to Louisiana or in Germany to Belgium. While these pipelines are located in Texas-Louisiana, most of the US-based FCVs are located in California.
