Case Study: the Toyota Mirai

A Study on the Function of a Hydrogen-Powered Car

The Toyota Mirai is one of the most innovative and popular Hydrogen-powered cars in the world and especially in Japan. Reputed as one of the best H2 cars, it was developed as a mid-high-end car for people looking to move away from traditional cars. Despite the relatively low number of cars manufacturerd compared to traditional gasoline or diesel-fueled cars, especially in Europe, the Toyota Mirai remains a headline in the future of Hydrogen-powered cars.
Left: Toyota Mirai

The Mirai's Hydrogen Tank is a great but still imperfect example of a solution to meet Hydrogen's storage needs.
The Toyota Mirai's Hydrogen tank is composed of a proprietary three-layer body:
  1. A plastic-lined interior layer - Di-hydrogen, the Hydrogen used in Hydrogen vehicles, is the smallest molecule currently known to man. It is therefore able to pass through many materials. This plastic layer achieves its purpose of storing the Hydrogen without allowing any leaks. The majority of the plastic used is UBE Nylon Resin, a plastic designed for Hydrogen permeation resistance and resistance to temperature changes (from refilling and discharging the H2 tanks), as well as shock resistance and other factors.
  2. Carbon fiber reinforced plastic layer - This layer is used to ensure pressure resistance while maintaining a low weight. It uses a new layering pattern to reduce the amount of carbon fiber used by approximately 40% compared to conventional hydrogen tanks
  3. Glass fiber-reinforced plastic layer- Designed to resist large impacts, this layer is located on the outside of the tank, and is able to withstand up to a .45 caliber gunshot from point-blank range (Similar to traditional revolver shot) or a high-speed crash 
However, many things could be improved about these tanks. Firstly, the Mirai's Hydrogen tanks are heavy- the 2 tanks used in a Mirai have a combined weight of 87.5kg but only store 5kg of H2. And most importantly, the Mirai's Hydrogen tanks need to increase in capacity. In fact, the average range on the Mirai is about 400 km. However, an average gasoline car will run for more than 550km. Combined with the lack of refueling stations, this limited range could prove bothersome. 
The Toyota Mirai also has one of the best fuel stack in the industry
As previously stated, some Hydrogen fuel cells utilize a way to circulate the Oxygen and Hydrogen into the fuel cell and the water out of it. In the Mirai's case, this is done through the use of what Toyota calls a 3D-Fine Mesh. It has outstanding performance in doing its role, contributing to the fact that the Mirai has the best fuel cell stacks in the industry. Another fact that contributes to these world-leading fuel cell stacks is the internal water circulation used. Fuel cells need to be constantly humid in order to function and not degrade. Normally, vehicles use the by-product of the electricity-generating reaction in order to humidify the fuel cell. However, Toyota has found a way, once again through the use of the 3-D mesh, to allow some water to remain inside of the fuel cell without its overhumidification, making the fuel cell stacks lighter and more compact. 

Toyota Mirai Fuel Cell (3-D FIne Mesh in Black)

Fuel cell Stacks inside the Mirai

However, some things about these fuel cell stacks can be improved. Firstly, each fuel cell requires platinum catalysts to split the Hydrogen and Oxygen molecules. Evidently, this needs to be improved due to the rarity and costs of Platinum. However, the replacement of Platinum is a topic currently being researched worldwide. Secondly, the fuel cells used in the Mirai, despite the fact that they are arguably the best today, are not the very efficient, and as said by Elon Musk, CEO of Tesla, an electric car manufacturing company, "running a fuel cell would be half as efficient as charging a battery pack directly... It's terrible. Why would you do that?". This should of course be taken with a grain of salt, considering Musk's position at Tesla, but he does have a point: the biggest problem with fuel cells is their lack of efficiency.
