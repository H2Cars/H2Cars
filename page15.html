Production of Hydrogen Through Natural Gas Reforming

Natural Gas Reforming is the most popular method of producing di-Hydrogen gas today. It involves the reaction of naturally-found methane (CH4) with steam (H20 gas).

Today, the most popular method of producing Hydrogen is through a process known as Natural Gas Reforming. It is estimated that in 2007, electrolysis of water to produce Hydrogen is only about 5% of the worldwide Hydrogen production, while natural gas reforming is a much greater percentage. The process takes place through these steps:

1) Methane and Steam are combined in a heated pressure-chamber.

2) After the chamber is heated to between ~15 bars, the Methane and Steam begin to react in a very endothermic reaction (hence, the heating). The equation for this reaction is as follows:

                                                                   CH4 + H2O → 3H2 + CO

3) The produced Hydrogen and Carbon monoxide can then be collected and stored, usually under pressure

4) In some cases, the Carbon Monoxide can be reused in another reaction with steam to produce more Hydrogen with the following reaction: 

                                                                    CO + H20 →  H2 + CO2

Evidently, this method of production of Hydrogen is a very energy-efficient and a low-cost method of production.  However, one problem with this is clear: it uses Methane as its primary reactant, which is one of the most harmful greenhouse gases. Through this process as well as when methane is obtained, greenhouse gases are released into the atmosphere, which would be an even bigger if Hydrogen were to become a more popular source of fuel. Moreover, the reaction between methane and water requires vast amounts of energy (ΔE = -206kJ/mol), which is also harmful to the planet.
