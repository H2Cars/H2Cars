Example for calculating range:

The VW Polo has a 40L fuel tank and has a fuel economy of 3.6L-6.1L/100km

(fuel_tank_cap(L) / fuel econ(L/100km)) * 100(km) = range

(40 / 3.6) * 100 = 1111    or (40 / 6.1) * 100 = 656

The VW Polo has a range going from approximately 660km to 1110km.

Analysis of characteristics

From these characteristics, we can deduce many things about the current state of hydrogen cars.

To begin with, we can surmise that lithium-ion battery cars score the lowest in terms of range and refuel/charging time. Internal combustion engine cars score well in terms of price; however, such vehicles are less effective in terms of recharging cost than lithium-ion battery cars (assuming the cheapest cost) but are slightly better than FCVs. In terms of charging time, lithium-ion battery cars are much slower, but conventional cars are similar to FCVs, but in terms of emissions, FCVs and EVs are the clear winners.

Indeed, hydrogen cars are more expensive than traditional internal combustion engines (as explained here in [economics of hydrogen cars]), especially if we do not take into account the externalities (such as pollution) associated with traditional cars. Hydrogen cars may be a better alternative than lithium-ion battery vehicles because of its longer range and its shorter refuel time. From the table above, their main problem as an alternative to replace traditional diesel/gasoline vehicles is the price of the car (which is influenced, in part, by the absence of mass production and the high cost of its components) and the price of its fuel, which is slightly higher than diesel/gasoline. From what was gathered from the information in this table, it is crucial for hydrogen cars to address their cost in order for them to be viable alternatives in replacing internal combustion engine cars.  

Alternatively, it is a frequent argument with other alternative energy choices that the market must take into account the externality costs of traditional technologies. In this case, the traditional vehicle which relies upon internal combustion engines, creates social costs, such as pollution, which are not fully expressed in the cost of a car purchased by a consumer. Accordingly, a proponent of hydrogen cars may argue that consumers who undertake the purchase of hydrogen cars should be rewarded (e.g., such as the generous subsidies offered to US and Western European consumers of electric automobiles) or the manufacturers of the traditional cars should be taxed to make alternatives more attractive.
