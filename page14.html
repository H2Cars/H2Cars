Production of Hydrogen through Electrolysis

The basic idea of this process is that pure water is put into a system that uses a battery to separate the Hydrogen atoms from the Oxygen atoms. This process uses a type of chemical reaction called an “oxidation-reduction reaction”, or redox reaction. This is a type of reaction that involves a transfer of electrons from one atom or molecule to another. It is also notable that this reaction cannot happen by itself and requires an electrical source to take place. This is how the process takes place: 


1) The system is composed of an anode and a cathode that are connected to the battery. The cathode is negatively charged, therefore has extra electrons, while the anode is positively charged, it needs electrons. Therefore, negatively-charged atoms will be attracted to the anode because they have extra electrons while the cathode attracts positively-charged atoms that don’t have enough electrons. A battery can be said to be an almost infinite storage space for electrons. Hence, the positive side of the battery can store thousands of electrons while the negative side has a huge amount of them already in store.


2) Distilled (pure) water is introduced in a container. Then, the cathode and anode are added. They are connected to the negative and positive side of the battery (that’s what makes gives them their charge, they are normally electrically neutral). At this point, the reaction can take place, but very slowly. An electric conductor is then added to the water. This can be substances such as Sulfuric Acid (H2SO4) or sometimes solid membranes like the ones used in Polymer Electrolyte Membranes, a popular type of electrolysis system used today. The reaction then begins to take place much quicker.

3) At this point, the chemical reaction is taking place much more quickly. The Hydrogen, which has an oxidation number of +1 in the compound H2O, is drawn to the cathode because the cathode can supply it with electrons to make it electrically neutral and more stable. There, it is joined with another Hydrogen atom from another water molecule and supplied with electrons to become H2 gas. This step is called reduction because the positively-charged atom gains electrons . On the other side (at the anode), the leftover molecule, OH- molecules (which are polyatomic ions), has an oxydation number of -1. The anode rips the electrons of multiple of these molecules, which leads to them becoming 2 water molecules and one O2 molecules. This step is called oxydation

This reaction can be modeled with the following equations:

Reduction at the Cathode:  2 H2O(l) + 2e−   →   H2(g) + 2 OH−(aq)
Oxidation at the anode:  2 OH−(aq)    →   1/2 O2(g) + H2O(l) + 2 e−

A word on the cathode and anode: 
Oxygen and Hydrogen are very electronegative, meaning that their electrons are pulled very closely and strongly to the molecule, making their molecules and electrons both very difficult to separate. In many cases, the cathodes and anodes are catalysts that help this process. Typically, they are made of materials like Platinum or a new compound made of gold, cobalt, and nickel that is said to be much more efficient than current catalysts materials.
