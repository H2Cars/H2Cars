Our experiment

Play the video after having read the description:

Close

The above captioned picture shows the components of our hydrogen vehicle. To recap, the primary purpose of this experiment is comparing the traits [characteristics] of hydrogen cars with those of internal combustion engine cars. This required us to calculate the energy efficiency of hydrogen and deduce aspects of such vehicles. 

To do this, we started by assembling the car. First, we connected the 3V battery to the reversible PEM fuel (half full of distilled water thanks to the syringe) and turned on the battery and then waited 15 minutes for it to electrolyze the water (in water containers), resulting in the division of the newly created H2 and 02 in different bells through tubes, which are separated from the water. After finishing the electrolysis process, we connected wires from the fuel cell to the motor and then after being drawn into the fuel cell, the H2 and O2 reacted together, and in doing so, created electricity which enabled the vehicle to move forward. 

We marked an "X" precisely under where the central wheel was at the beginning of the movement and then we marked an "X" at the end of its movement. We then measured the distance travelled by the vehicle and additionally measured the levels of H2 and 02 before and after (see table below). This allowed us to perform the calculations required to compare hydrogen cars with other vehicles.

A note about the results:
Evidently, our results are strange. Firstly, if we look at the equation for the electrolysis done, we should have double the Hydrogen produced than Oxygen produced. However, this is not the case. Moreover, the opposite of this reaction would mean that we use double the Hydrogen than Oxygen. However, this is once again not the case. We were told that the car was old and had been left without use for a long time. This is most likely the source of these inaccurate results. However, we still managed to hypothesize multiple assertions.

To conclude, this experiment showed us multiple things. First, it proved to us that Hydrogen is much less energetically efficient than gasoline cars. In fact, we see from our results that 1 kilogram of Hydrogen was about 3 times less efficient than 1 kilogram of gasoline. However, our experiment proved one even greater thing: the need to compress Hydrogen in cars. Evidently, our car did not go very far with the amount of Hydrogen that it had in store. In fact, the average distance traveled was 224.4 cm. This shows that uncompressed Hydrogen has very little value for Hydrogen cars because it cannot provide the range needed for Hydrogen vehicles. However, one thing that we noticed from our results was that many times we still had leftover Hydrogen. One hypothesis for why this happened was that our fuel cell had been left dry for too long, which could have led to it being so degraded that it could not use all of its surface to produce electricity. Therefore, once the amount of Hydrogen began to go down, less Hydrogen was able to be used in the reaction, so the car stopped going forwards. One other fact that could back up this hypothesis was that if we picked up the car after it stopped moving, the wheels powering it would start turning again. 

The odd results from our experiment show one final disadvantage of H2 cars: if left unattended for too long, the fuel cell stacks in Hydrogen vehicles would degrade and the car could stop working. Moreover, this poses the question of lifespan of a Hydrogen-powered vehicle: can a car running on current fuel cell technology run for as long as a gasoline combustion engine, or would the fuel cell stacks need to be changed? Perhaps one way to test this would be to give a Hydrogen-powered car an unlimited supply of Hydrogen and Oxygen, and then to see if the fuel cell stacks ever degraded enough that the car would stop moving.
