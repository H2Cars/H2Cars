Fuel Cell Stacks

H2＋½O2→H2O

Fuel Cell Stacks convert stored Di-Hydrogen into electricity by way of an oxidation-reduction reaction between stored Hydrogen and Oxygen naturally present in the air to form H2O.

A single fuel cell only has a voltage of about 1 volt. Therefore, fuel cells are stacked in series by the hundreds to increase the voltage and power output. Each cell requires several things:

  • A CATHODE AND AN ANODE CATALYST - The Hydrogen is split and loses its electrons to become H+ ions at the anode while the O2 is split at the cathode. The H+ ions (protons) flow through the membrane while the Hydrogen electrons flow through the car, powering the motor. Today, these use Platinum because it is the most efficient catalyst that can be found. 
  • AN ELECTROLYTE MEMBRANE - This is where the Hydrogen ions flow through towards the oxygen where they combine to create H20. These membranes require multiple things: they first need to be electron-proof to prevent electrons from short-circuiting the system. Furthermore, they need to be gas-proof in order to prevent gas crossover, where the O2and H2 combine to produce water without generating electricity
  • OPTIONAL: METHOD TO TRANSPORT O2, H2, H2O - Modern cars such as the Toyota Mirai use a 3D Fine Mesh to increase the efficiency of the flow of Hydrogen, Oxygen, and Water to allow for much more efficient spread of the reactants throughout the fuel cell, therefore increasing production as well as easier evacuation of the water produced.                                Diagram of a PEM Fuel Cell, the most commonly used type in                                                      Hydrogen-powered vehicles
